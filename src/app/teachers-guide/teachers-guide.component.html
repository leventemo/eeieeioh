Activities

play_circle

We're working on a variety of activities; they're going to be the backbone of eeieeioh. Each of them are designed for students to do different things with English. They each require a specific "setup" of how many learners should do them (learners on their own, in pairs, small groups or as a whole class), who has a phone with the prompts and how they are suppese to use them, is there a need for a projector, etc. When opening an activity, click on theinfo_outline icon for instructions.

Cards

The simplest activity of all: students press a button, get some prompt, then do something. As a teacher, I kind of enjoy not having to photocopy, cut or sort cards into a given number of pairs/groups before class any more. Ever. Try demo.

Timed Cards

These are Cards, as seen above, but with a timer: teachers sometimes want to restrict or stretch the time they expect their students to work with each single card. Try demo.

Expandables

Our expandables are not expendable as they are a tool for eliciting ideas. If done as a whole class with a projector in use, it gives the teacher control over the moment the hidden text is revealed. It might work with autonomous learners going over their lists on their phones as well. The activity was designed for error correction, as a prelude to Duels, which is a fast-paced game. We tend to use the two together. Try demo.

Reword

Aiming at recycling and internalising chunks: fixed phrases, idioms, time expressions, dependent prepositions, etc. Players get 2 points for rephrasing a sentence on their own or 1 point for doing the same after getting a clue, which is a keyword from the target phrase. Try demo.

Duels

Not unlike Cards but it has timers and counters for 2 players who take turns to quickly chose between a correct and an incorrect form of a word, a phrase or anything really. Designed for quick and somewhat personalized error correction. It works best when the same content is done on Expandables first. Try demo.

Escape Room

Played individually, each student on their own phone – or in pairs, one phone per pair. This activity is about accuracy: players read short prompts and type their responses. They get an average of the time they've spent on each question. They are not allowed to move on to the text question until they get the present one correct. Ideal for spelling but works well with anything that requires short responses, e.g. dependent prepositions. Try demo.

Context

This activity is quite like Duels, at least in terms of functionality. However, the idea here is that the prompts players are shown on each card will create their own (lexical, grammatical, functional, etc.) context as a group. Players are then asked to decide which one of the given options belongs to the perceived context. It's an efficient tool for recycling large lexical sets but it also works well as a framework for "collocation forks" or any type of grouping game. At the end of the game players are shown feedback in the form of a detailed log of the game which lists all the words in the prompts so this activity might even work well as the first T in what we call the "Test-Teach-Test" procedure. Try demo.

Ranking

A simple interface for what the name suggests: a ranking task where learners express opinions, negotiate and make decisions together. It works well as a Pyramid Discussion but it's also a common speaking task at language exams. Try demo.

Board Game

This one works best with strict structures which call for those "Finish the sentence"-type of exercises such as verb patterns and conditionals. Learners need to follow strict grammar rules but they are given open-ended mini stories to develop in their own way. They are also given the power – and responsibility – to assess their partner's solutions, which might foster learner autonomy and collaboration. Try demo.
